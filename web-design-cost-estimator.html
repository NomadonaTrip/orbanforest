What goes into the cost of a website?

Website pricing in Ontario varies widely. A template-based brochure site for a local business in Paris, ON might cost $1,000, while a custom-built e-commerce platform for a manufacturer in Kitchener-Waterloo could be $20,000+. Here's what drives the numbers.

Website type

A simple informational site is far less complex than an online store or a custom web application with user accounts and dashboards. The more your site needs to do, the more it costs to build.

Design complexity

Template-based designs ($300 - $1,500) use pre-built layouts and are the fastest to launch. Semi-custom sites ($1,500 - $5,000) start with a framework but add strategic custom sections. Fully custom builds ($3,500 - $18,000+) are designed and coded from scratch to match your brand exactly.

Page count and content

More pages means more design, development, and content work. In this market, additional pages typically add $50 - $150 each. If you need professional copywriting (rather than providing your own content), budget an additional $35 - $100 per page.

Features and integrations

Booking systems, AI chatbots, client portals, payment processing, CRM integrations, and multilingual support all add significant development time. Each feature is essentially a project within the project.

Ongoing costs

After launch, your website needs hosting ($10 - $50/month), maintenance and security updates ($60 - $200/month), and a domain renewal ($15 - $25/year). A reasonable monthly budget for a small business site in Ontario is $75 - $225.

Why freelancer rates?

In the Brantford, Hamilton, and KWC market, freelancers and small studios charge $35 - $80/hr — 30 to 40% less than traditional agencies ($50 - $130/hr). You get the same quality work with lower overhead. This estimator uses freelancer-level rates because that's what most small businesses in this region actually pay. This region is also 15 - 30% less expensive than Toronto.

When the cheapest option costs more

A $500 website that doesn't convert visitors into customers is more expensive than a $3,500 website that pays for itself in three months. The right question isn't "what's the cheapest price?" — it's "what will this investment return?"
